# Bring Your App to ChatGPT

*A plain-English guide for people who saw the phrase "bring your app to ChatGPT" and thought: cool, but what does that actually mean?*

---

## The short version

This is about making your app usable inside ChatGPT.

Not "ChatGPT replaces your app." More like: people can open ChatGPT, ask for something, and your app can show up there with the right actions, buttons, or results.

If that sounds vague, that's because the idea is broader than the name. The useful version is:

- ChatGPT can reach your app
- Your app can do a job for the person
- The person does not need to leave the chat to get the result

---

## What a normal person should picture

Imagine someone asks ChatGPT:

- "Book me a table"
- "Check my account"
- "Show me my latest report"
- "Draft a reply in my app"

Instead of ChatGPT just talking about it, your app can actually help do the thing.

So if you have:

- a booking app
- a dashboard
- a support tool
- a shopping app
- a workflow app

this use case is about making that app available inside ChatGPT in a useful, controlled way.

---

## What you do not need to know

- You do not need to know what an SDK is to understand the goal.
- You do not need to read a huge technical document first.
- You do not need to become an engineer to tell someone what the app should do.

If you're the person who knows the business problem, that's already useful.

---

## What this is really for

This is a good fit when you want:

- your app to be reachable from ChatGPT
- a simple user flow that can start in chat and finish in your app
- a focused action, not a giant rework
- a way for people to ask for something in plain language and get the right app action

It is not just "put our brand in ChatGPT." It needs to solve a real task.

---

## The simplest way to think about the build

You usually need three things:

1. A clear job for the app to do
2. A way for ChatGPT to talk to that app
3. A simple interface or result the person can use

That might sound technical, but the human version is:

- what should the person ask for
- what should happen next
- what should they see or do

---

## Good examples

Good:

- "Let people check their orders inside ChatGPT."
- "Let support staff pull up a customer record from chat."
- "Let someone draft a message using their saved brand settings."
- "Let a manager ask for a weekly summary and get it from the app."

Too vague:

- "Make our app work with AI."
- "Integrate ChatGPT."
- "Add an AI feature."

Those sound fancy and say almost nothing.

---

## If you are briefing someone else

Try this:

```text
We want people to do [job] from inside ChatGPT.
The person should be able to ask for [thing] in plain English.
The app should return [result] or take [action].
Keep it simple and focused on one task first.
```

Example:

```text
We want customers to check the status of their latest order from inside ChatGPT.
They should be able to ask in plain English.
The app should return the current status, tracking info, and next steps.
Start with one flow and keep it easy to test.
```

---

## Questions worth answering early

- Who is the person using this?
- What is the one job we want them to do?
- What information does the app need?
- What should happen if something goes wrong?
- What should the person see at the end?
- What is the smallest useful version?

If you can answer those, you are already ahead of most "AI integration" conversations.

---

## The trap to avoid

The trap is trying to make it sound bigger than it is.

If the first plan is ten features, three departments, and a moonshot vision, it will probably become expensive and confusing.

Start with one useful action that people would actually use.

---

## Plain-English summary

This use case is about letting people do a useful thing from inside ChatGPT using your app.

That is it.

Make the job clear, keep the first version small, and make sure a normal person can understand what they are supposed to ask for.
